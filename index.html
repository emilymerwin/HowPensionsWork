There are two kinds of pensions

  • defined contribution
    Workers in government plans may have to pay a percentage of their salaries toward the pension system each paycheck. The government also makes an Annual Required Contribution, which is calculated by a complex formula that takes into consideration the annual normal pension costs and the money needed to close the funding gap within the period of time set by the pension board.
  • defined benefit
    A defined benefit plan guarantees an employee will receive a specific sum of money each month once retired. That sum is typically calculated based on years of service and the average salary earned over the last years of work.

The money is usually invested

  • in stocks, bonds and other commodities. The earnings on those investments help pay benefits.

Gains or losses

  • with those investments are typically averaged over a five- or 10-year period so that there are not wild swings in the amount governments have to contribute. This practice is called “smoothing.”

Pension plans are typically tied to an employee's tenure at a company.

  • If an employee makes payments over a given time, they are said to be vested, which means the benefit is guaranteed by the employer and cannot be take away even if the employee leaves the company.

As people retire,

  • they begin to draw out cash from the system while active employees continue paying into it.
